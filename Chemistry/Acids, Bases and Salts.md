## Acids
### Properties of Acids
- They have a sour taste.
- They react with *active metals*.
- They react with carbonates to produce $CO_2$.
- They change color of vegetable dyes.
- They react with bases to form ionic salts (Neutralization).
### Structure of Acids
#### Binary Acids
Binary acids have acid hydrogens attached to a nonmetal atom.
- $HCl$
- $HF$
#### Oxyacids
Oxyacids have acid hydrogens attached to an oxygen atom.
- $H_2SO_4$
- $HNO_3$
#### Organic Acids
Organic acids have *carboxylic acid* functional group $(COOH)$. They normally behave as *weak acids*.
- Citric acid
- Malic acid
- Formic acid
## Bases
### Properties of Bases
- They are also known as *alkalis*.
- They have a bitter taste.
- Their Solutions feels slippery.
- They change color of vegetable dyes (different color than acids).
- They react with acids to form ionic salts (Neutralization).
### Structure of Bases
#### Ionic Bases
Most ionic bases contain $OH^-$ ions but some contain $CO_3^{2-}$ ions.
- $NaOH$
- $Ca(OH)_2$
- $CaCO_3$
- $NaHCO_3$
#### Organic Bases
Organic bases contain structures that react with $H^+$. They are mostly *amine groups* functional group. They normally behave as *weak bases*.
- Caffeine
## Acid-Base Theories
### Arrhenius Theory
- Acids *ionize* in water to produce H+ ions and anions.
	- $HCl \to H^+ + Cl^-$
	- Since $H^+$ is very *reactive* they can't exist in water so they react with water to produce $H_3O$. 
- Bases *dissociate* in water to produce $OH^-$ ions and cations.
	- $NaOH \to Na^+ + OH^-$
**NOTE:** This theory can't explain $NH_3, CO_3^{2-},...$
#### Arrhenius Acid and Base Reactions
- The $H^+$ from the acid combines with $OH^-$ from the base to make a molecule of $H_2O$.
- The cation from the base combines with the anion from the acid to make a salt.
$$
HCL_{\mathrm{(aq)}} + NaOH_{\mathrm{(aq)}} \to NaCl_{\mathrm{(aq)}} + H_2O_{\mathrm{(l)}}
$$
### Br√∏nsted-Lowry Theory
- Acids are $H^+$ *donors*.
	- $HCl + H_2O \to H_3O^+ + Cl^-$
	- Any substance that has $H$ can be potentially a Br√∏nsted-Lowry acid. Because often $H$ is easy to transfer.
- Bases are $H^+$ *acceptors*.
	- $NH_3 + H_2O \rightleftarrows NH_4^+ + OH^-$
	- Any substance that has atoms with lone pairs can be potentially a Br√∏nsted-Lowry base. Because often those are more willing to accept $H$ transfer than others.
**NOTE:** In this theory water can act both as an acid and as a base.
#### Br√∏nsted-Lowry Acid and Base Reactions
In an acid-base reaction, the acid molecule gives an H+ to the base molecule. So we can generalize this like:
$$
AH + B \rightleftarrows A^- + BH^+
$$
$AH \to$ acid
$B \to$ base
$A^- \to$ conjugate base
$BH^+ \to$ conjugate acid

## Acidic and Basic Solutions
All aqueous solutions contain both $H_3O^+$ and $OH^‚Äì$ ions.
- $[H_3O^+] = [OH^-] = 10^{-7} \to$ Neutral Solutions
- $[H_3O^+] > 10^{-7},\ [OH^-] < 10^{-7} \to$ Acidic Solutions
- $[H_3O^+] < 10^{-7},\ [OH^-] > 10^{-7} \to$ Basic Solutions
## The $pH$ Scale
The acidity/basicity of a solution is often expressed as pH.
- $pH = -\mathrm{log}([H_3O^+])$
- $[H_3O^+] = 10^{-pH}$

- $pH = 7 \to$ Neutral
- $pH < 7 \to$ Acidic
- $pH > 7 \to$ Basic
### $pOH$
$pOH$ is the complete inverse of $pH$.
- $pOH = -\mathrm{log}([OH^-])$
- $[OH^-] = 10^{-pOH}$
![[Pasted image 20240102103319.png]]
### Relationship between $pH$ and $pOH$
At $25\degree C$:
$$
pH + pOH = 14
$$
![[Pasted image 20240102103442.png]]
### $pK$
Another way of expressing the strength of an acid or base is $pK$.
- $pK_a = -\mathrm{log}(K_a)$
- $K_a = 10^{-pK_a}$
- $pK_b = -\mathrm{log}(K_b)$
- $K_b = 10^{-pK_b}$
**The stronger the acid, the smaller the $pK_a$**
So, larger $K_a \to$ smaller $pK_a$
### The Conjugate Seesaw
$$
K_a \cdot K_b = K_w
$$
$$
pK_a + pK_b = pK_w
$$
So at $25\degree C$:
$$
pK_a + pK_b = 14
$$
