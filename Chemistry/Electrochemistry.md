## Redox Reaction
Redox reactions are very important part of the electrochemistry, so you have to know about them well.
In Redox Reactions:
- One or more elements change oxidation number.
- Always have both oxidation and reduction.
![[Pasted image 20240114204648.png]]
### Oxidation & Reduction
**Oxidation** is a process that occurs when:
- Oxidation number of an element *increases*.
- Element loses electron.
- Compound adds oxygen.
- Compound loses hydrogen.
- Half-Reaction has electrons as products.
**Reduction** is a process that occurs when:
- Oxidation number of an element *decreases*.
- Element gains electron.
- Compound loses oxygen.
- Compound gains hydrogen.
- Half-Reaction has electrons as reactants.

### Oxidizing Agent & Reducing Agent
- The reactant that *oxidizes* an element in another reactant is called the *oxidizing agent*. So the oxidizing agent contains the element that is *reduced*.
- the reactant that *reduces* an element in another reactant is called the *reducing agent*. So the reducing agent contains the element that is *oxidized*.

## Electrical Current
Electric current is the amount of electric charge that passes a point in a given period of time (whether as electrons flowing through a wire or ions flowing through a solution).

Redox reactions involve the transfer of electrons from one substance to another. Therefore, redox reactions have the *potential* to generate an electric current.
In order to use that current, we need to separate the place where oxidation is occurring from the place that reduction is occurring.
## Electrochemical Cells
- The conversion between chemical energy and electrical energy is carried out in an *electrochemical cell*.
- Spontaneous redox reactions take place in a *voltaic cell (galvanic cell)*
- Non-spontaneous redox reactions can be made to occur in an *electrolytic cell* by the addition of electrical energy.
### Voltaic Cells (Galvanic Cell)
![[Pasted image 20240114234956.png]]
- The oxidation occurs at the *anode*. (-)
- The reduction occurs at the *cathode*. (+)
- Salt bridge, usually a U-shaped tube that contains a salt solution, to keep the charges balanced.
- Cations move towards the cathode.
- Anions move towards the anode.
- Electrons leave the anode and flow through the wire to the cathode.

Once even one electron flows from the anode to the cathode, the charges in each beaker would not be balanced and the flow of electrons would stop.
Therefore, we use a *salt bridge*, usually a U-shaped tube that contains a salt solution, to keep the charges balanced.
#### Daniell Cell (Can be asked in exam)
Daniell Cell is a type of Voltaic Cell (Galvanic cell).
We have this spontaneous reaction:
$$
Zn_{(s)} + Cu^{2+}_{(aq)} \to Cu_{(s)} + Zn^{2+}_{(aq)}
$$
Oxidation Half Reaction:
$$
Zn_{(s)} \to Zn^{2+} + 2e^-
$$
Reduction Half Reaction:
$$
Cu^{2+} + 2e^- \to Cu_{(s)}
$$

## Current and Voltage
![[Pasted image 20240114235726.png]]
- Likewise, electrons only spontaneously flow one way in a redox reaction from higher to lower potential energy.
- Similarly to a falling weight, the maximum work that an electron can do is equal to its charge times the difference in electrical potential through which it falls.

### Current
- The number of electrons that flow through the system per second.
- Unit: Ampere
- 1 A of current = 1 Coulomb of charge flowing by each second
- Electrode surface area dictates the number of electrons that can flow
### Potential Difference $(E)$
- The difference in potential energy between the reactants and products.
- Unit: Volt
- The voltage needed to drive electrons through the external circuit.
- Amount of force pushing the electrons through the wire is called the *electromotive force (emf)*
$$
\Delta G_r = -nFE
$$
- $n \to$ Stoichiometric coefficient of the reaction
- $F \to$ Faraday’s constant $\to$ $9.6485 \cdot 10^4 (C / \mathrm{mol}\ e^-)$
	- You need to know Faraday's constant for exercises.
- $E \to$ Potential difference
	- The potential difference under these conditions is the *maximum* potential that can be produced. It is called the *cell potential* (which is still widely called the electromotive force, emf, of the cell)
