## What is Dynamics
- Kinematics explains *how* objects move.
- Dynamics explains *why* objects move (what forces make them move).
## Fundamental Forces
1) Electromagnetic Forces
2) Gravitational Forces
3) Weak Nuclear Forces
4) Strong Nuclear Forces
**NOTE:** They're all *field forces*. That means they don't require contact.
## Newton's First Law of Motion (The Law of Inertia)
> Every object continues in its state of rest, or uniform velocity in a straight line, as long as no net force is acting on it.
### Mass and Inertia
*Inertia* is the tendency of an object to remain in its current state. So we can say *mass* is a way to measure *inertia*.
In the *SI*, mass is measured in $kg$.
## Newton's Second Law of Motion
> The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the force acting on it, and it is inversely proportional to the object’s mass. the direction of the acceleration is the same of that of the force acting on it.
$$
\vec{a} = \frac{\sum{\vec{F}}}{m}
$$
$$
\sum{\vec{F}} = m\vec{a}
$$
### Gravitational Force
$$
\vec{F_g} = m\vec{g}
$$
Where $g$ is the gravitational constant and equal to $9.80 \frac{m}{s^2}$

## Newton's Third Law of Motion
> Whenever an object exerts a force on another object, the second object exerts a force equal and of opposite direction to the first.
> "To each action there is an equal and opposite reaction"
$$
\vec{F_1} = -\vec{F_2}
$$

**NOTE:** These two forces, although present in the system at the same time, cannot be added as $\sum{\vec{F}} = m \vec{a}$ because *they act on two different objects*.

### Free Body Diagrams
When solving a problem which involves multiple forces acting on an object it's a good idea to draw free body diagrams.
To draw a free body diagram:
- Select your object. (If there are more than one object draw free body diagram for each of them)
- Draw all the forces acting on that object. (Do not draw the forces that the object is exerting on other objects)
- Separate all the forces on it's $x$ and $y$ components.
